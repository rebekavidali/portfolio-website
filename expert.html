Expert

Expert is a responsive web app that connects users with home repair professionals by providing a smooth home visit booking experience, video calls and support from community.

How it started

Challenge

My initial challenge was to create an app for users to contact home repair experts when they need advice because they want to reduce their costs with home visits. But the web app was supposed to be designed for the Slovenian market, where ‘’everybody knows everybody’’ and they always have someone to turn to for advice. During interviews I realized a completely different need immerged: to have a platform to quickly find a reliable expert for a home visit.

Problem statement

Expert users need an efficient way to book home visits from home repair experts when they need help to save time and reduce frustration of making several phone calls.

Research

Competitive analysis

At first, I looked at some of the video call booking websites for home DIY experts like TheOne and DIY Coach, later I also analyzed AirBnB and Booking for their booking experience and Mojmojster.si - a local website for finding home repairmen.

A quick survey

I conducted a survey to collect some preliminary results. Here are the key insights:

  • Most participants try to fix broken things alone and they google for instructions
  • Most participants find experts through word of mouth
  • The most common way of contacting the experts is by calling them
  • The most important criteria for choosing a home repair expert are recommendations/reviews.

User interviews

The user inverviews were done with 3 participants from Slovenia, aged between 26 and 49. Their level of experience with home professionals was from ''home repair noob'' to ''I like to do things alone''.

Here are the most important insights:

  • DIY: Interviewees try to do smaller home repairs alone, for more serious issues they hire a professional.
  • IT’S HARD: Finding a professional is very hard in Slovenia since there are few and they can be very unreliable.
  • TRUST IS KEY: They like to ask around for contacts as they find references to be the most important thing when hiring an expert.
  • GOOD SERVICE: They would rather pay more than receive bad service.

Sitemaps

First Sitemap

The first idea was to make a landing page and have people log in in order to access the dashboard, that would then allow them to search for experts and post to the community.

Iterated Sitemap

After conducting an open card sort, I realized that it’s better to let users have the opportunity to search for an expert or browse community and offer a log in/sign up only when they decide to book or post.

User Personas

Based on data gathered from user research, I’ve created two different user personas - Ina and Matej. To help me empathize with them, I imagined real-life scenarios where they’d use the web app.

Wireframing

Low Fidelity

The first idea was to have the search on the homepage and the user would pick the time the expert was available when they decided to book the home visit.

Mid-Fidelity

In mid-fidelity version the 4 CTAs on the expert profile page were grouped into ‘‘help online’’ and ‘‘come over’’.

High Fidelity

After some quick usability testing of the mid-fidelity flow (3 participants), it became apparent that the users didn’t understand what ‘help online’ and ‘come over’ meant, so I reintroduced the 4 CTAs, this time with a different set of icons. I also removed search from homepage to avoid cognitive overload and created a separate search page with ‘‘select time’’ option was included.

User testing

Expert was tested with 6 moderated tests (1 in-person, 5 remote via Zoom) - 5 via computer, 1 on the tablet. Participants were varied, from 19 years old student to a 52-year-old in the military. Although they all have different stories, they have one thing in common: they were all Slovenian.

Key insights:

  • 2/6 participants didn’t know how to go to community

    Solution: Change ‘Visit community’ text to ‘Go to community’ and separate the button from the background

  • 4/6 users didn’t find the button to post to community

    Solution: The button was placed underneath the ‘Welcome to the community’ text and the search icon at the upper right corner

  • 4/6 participants started scrolling the list of possible topics instead of using search

    Solution: Make the search more prominent, add commonly used topics as chips

  • 4/6 participants participants had muestions about how payments work

    Solution: Add a feature to pay upfront or after the job is done, create a booking timeline

  • 3/6 participants think the app is not useful for emergencies

    Solution: add an emergency feature, add a phone number to the expert profile

Refinement

Design System

The solutions were implemented and the high fidelity prototype was slowly starting to see the light of day. For quicker building, I developed a design system and used it faithfully throughout the app. Visual design was tested by my peers and it went through several iterations.

Finishing Touches

I also implemented the accessibility features. The web app originally had a light green color palette, which was later changed to dark green to comply with WCAG contrast standards. I also added form labels and the placeholders were now examples.

Final Solution

Takeaways

What Did I Learn?

This was my first project and, while it was certainly not easy, I had a lot of fun and learned A TON. User research and testing thought me to stay open-minded since I never know what I might discover. Mistakes will be made and that’s okay. That’s why we test and ask questions in the first place.

What's Next?

Expert was inspired by me moving to another country, a bigger city than my little Koper, getting locked out of my apartment at 9 p.m. and not knowing a single locksmith to turn to for help. Moving forward I’d like to develop the expert side of the web app to improve the booking experience even more.
